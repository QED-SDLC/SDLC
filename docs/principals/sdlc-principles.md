## SDLC Principles
The following principles guide all software delivery across the organisation. These principles can be applied regardless of delivery methodology (Waterfall, Agile, or Product Operating Model).

### Business Value First
All work must be aligned to clearly defined business outcomes. Delivery teams are accountable for ensuring that effort is prioritised based on value, risk, and strategic alignment.

* Every initiative should define measurable success criteria
* Work should be continuously reprioritised based on value
* Non-value-adding activities should be minimised

---

### Security and Privacy by Design
Security, privacy, and regulatory compliance must be embedded throughout the lifecycle, not treated as afterthoughts.

* Security requirements must be identified during planning and design
* Secure coding and testing practices must be followed
* Solutions must comply with organisational and regulatory standards

---

### Shift Left
Risk identification, testing, and validation activities should occur as early as possible.

* Early validation reduces cost and rework
* Privacy, Policy, Security and Architecture advice should be sought during the discovery process
* Prototyping and early testing are strongly encouraged
* Quality and risk are addressed continuously, not deferred

---

### Iterative and Incremental Delivery
Solutions should be delivered in manageable increments to enable feedback and continuous improvement.

* Frequent delivery reduces risk and increases adaptability
* Feedback loops must be built into delivery processes
* Large, high-risk releases should be avoided where possible

---

### Automation First
Automation should be the default approach for build, test, deployment, and operational processes.

* Reduces manual error and increases consistency
* Improves speed and repeatability
* Enables scalable and reliable delivery pipelines

---

### Traceability and Transparency
All artefacts across the SDLC must be traceable and visible.

* Requirements, design, code, testing, and releases must be linked
* Decisions and changes must be auditable
* Stakeholders must have visibility of progress and risks

---

### Quality is Built-In
Quality is a shared responsibility across all roles, not owned solely by testing functions.

* Definition of “done” must include quality criteria
* Defects should be prevented, not just detected
* Continuous improvement practices must be embedded

---

### Clear Accountability and Ownership
All aspects of delivery must have clearly defined ownership.

* Roles and responsibilities must be explicit
* Decision-making authority must be understood
* Accountability cannot be delegated without clarity

---

### Governance with Guardrails (Flexible but Controlled)
The SDLC supports multiple delivery approaches while maintaining strong governance through clearly defined, non-negotiable controls.

**Key Principle:**
*Flexibility is enabled within boundaries—not in place of them.*

#### Mandatory Controls (Non-Negotiable)
The following must always be adhered to, regardless of methodology:

* Security and compliance requirements
* Change and release management controls
* Documentation of key decisions and artefacts
* Traceability of requirements through to deployment
* Appropriate testing and validation
* Risk identification and management

#### Flexible Implementation (Context-Driven)
Teams are empowered to tailor *how* they meet these controls based on:

* Delivery methodology (e.g., Agile, Waterfall, Product)
* Risk profile (low vs high criticality systems)
* Complexity and scale
* Regulatory or business requirements

Examples:

* Documentation may be lightweight (Agile) or formal (Waterfall), but must exist
* Testing may be automated-first or include formal test phases, but must be demonstrable
* Governance checkpoints may be embedded in ceremonies (Agile) or stage gates (Waterfall)

#### Guardrails to Maintain Governance Integrity
To ensure flexibility does not weaken governance:

* Tailoring decisions must be **intentional and documented**
* Teams must demonstrate **equivalent or better control outcomes**
* High-risk initiatives require **increased governance, not less**
* Deviations from standard practices must be **reviewed and approved where required**
* All approaches must remain **auditable and transparent**

#### Outcome-Based Governance
Governance is assessed based on outcomes, not process rigidity:

* Are risks identified and managed?
* Is the solution secure and compliant?
* Is quality demonstrable?
* Is the system supportable and maintainable?

If these outcomes are not met, the approach—regardless of methodology—is not acceptable.

---

### Continuous Improvement
The SDLC itself is subject to ongoing review and enhancement.

* Teams are encouraged to identify and implement improvements
* Metrics and feedback must inform change
* Lessons learned must be captured and shared
