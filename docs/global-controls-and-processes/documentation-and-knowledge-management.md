## Documentation & Knowledge Management
The Documentation & Knowledge Management phase ensures that all relevant information about software, systems, and processes is captured, maintained, and made accessible to the right stakeholders. Effective documentation reduces risk, supports operational efficiency, and enables new team members or external parties to understand and work with the system confidently.

---

### Objectives
The objectives of Documentation & Knowledge Management are to:

* Preserve institutional knowledge about systems, processes, and design decisions
* Provide clear and accurate guidance for users, developers, and operators
* Support compliance, auditing, and governance requirements
* Enable effective handovers between teams, vendors, and external partners
* Reduce reliance on tribal knowledge

---

### Key Activities
#### Requirements and Design Documentation
* Maintain up-to-date documentation of requirements, designs, and prototypes
* Ensure traceability between requirements, design artefacts, and delivered functionality
* Capture design decisions, trade-offs, and rationale for future reference

---

#### Technical Documentation
* Document system architecture, interfaces, APIs, and dependencies
* Provide detailed build, deployment, and configuration instructions
* Include troubleshooting guides, error handling procedures, and monitoring expectations
* Keep documentation versioned and aligned with system changes

---

#### User Documentation
* Provide guidance for end-users, administrators, and support teams
* Include:

  * User manuals
  * Quick reference guides
  * FAQs and knowledge articles
* Ensure documentation is understandable, accurate, and accessible

---

#### Knowledge Capture and Sharing
* Encourage teams to record lessons learned and best practices
* Facilitate knowledge sharing sessions, wikis, or internal portals
* Use knowledge management tools to store and categorise information
* Update knowledge artefacts iteratively to reflect changes

---

#### Versioning and Maintenance
* Ensure documentation is versioned alongside software releases
* Retire outdated or obsolete documentation
* Maintain clear ownership for all documentation artefacts

---

### Outputs and Deliverables
Typical outputs include:

* Requirements and design documentation
* Architecture and interface specifications
* Build, deployment, and configuration guides
* User manuals, training materials, and FAQs
* Knowledge base articles and lessons learned logs
* Version history and update logs

---

### Governance and Controls
Key governance requirements:

* **Ownership**: Each documentation artefact must have a responsible owner
* **Accessibility**: Documentation must be stored in an approved, accessible repository
* **Version Control**: Changes to documentation must be tracked and auditable
* **Alignment**: Documentation must reflect the current state of the system
* **Review and Approval**: Critical documentation must be reviewed and approved

**Control Outcome:**
Documentation and knowledge assets are **accurate, accessible, and maintainable**, supporting operational continuity and compliance.

---

### Tailoring by Delivery Model
* **Agile / Product Models**:

  * Documentation is concise, “just enough,” and updated iteratively
  * Emphasis on knowledge sharing and wikis over static manuals

* **Waterfall**:

  * Comprehensive documentation is created upfront and maintained through the lifecycle
  * Formal reviews and approvals are standard

---

### Proportionality and Risk-Based Approach
The level of documentation should be proportionate to:

* System criticality and complexity
* Regulatory and compliance requirements
* Expected turnover or external usage

Examples:

* Enterprise-critical systems require full technical and user documentation
* Low-risk internal tools may require only lightweight guides or internal wiki pages

---

### Anti-Patterns to Avoid
* Creating documentation once and letting it go stale
* Relying on tribal knowledge without written guidance
* Over-documenting trivial changes that are unlikely to impact users or maintainers
* Failing to version or review documentation alongside system updates

---

### Closing Statement

Effective Documentation & Knowledge Management ensures that systems are understandable, maintainable, and auditable. It preserves critical knowledge, reduces risk, and empowers teams to deliver value consistently and confidently.
