## Lifecycle Overview
The Software Development Lifecycle (SDLC) defines the end-to-end process for delivering software, from initial concept through to deployment and ongoing support. It provides a consistent structure that supports multiple delivery methodologies while ensuring alignment with organisational standards, governance, and risk controls.

### End-to-End Lifecycle
The SDLC consists of the following core phases:

1. **Plan & Discover** – Define the problem, explore solutions, and validate assumptions (including prototyping in Product Operating Model contexts)
2. **Design** – Develop solution architecture, user experience, and technical designs
3. **Build & Integrate** – Develop, configure, and integrate solution components
4. **Test & Validate** – Verify that the solution meets functional and non-functional requirements
5. **Release & Deploy** – Deliver the solution into production environments
6. **Operate & Improve** – Monitor, support, and continuously enhance the solution

These phases may be executed sequentially (e.g., Waterfall) or iteratively (e.g., Agile and Product models), but all must be addressed in every initiative.

---

### Methodology-Agnostic Structure
This SDLC is designed to support multiple delivery approaches:

* **Waterfall**: Phases are typically completed in sequence with formal stage gates
* **Agile (e.g., Scrum)**: Phases are iterative and occur continuously within sprints
* **Product Operating Model (POM)**: Emphasises continuous discovery, delivery, and improvement with persistent teams

Regardless of methodology:

* All SDLC phases must be represented
* Mandatory controls and governance requirements must be met
* Outcomes must be demonstrable and auditable

---

### Iterative and Continuous Nature
While the SDLC is presented as a series of phases, in practice it is **non-linear and iterative**:

* Activities such as design, build, and testing often occur concurrently
* Feedback loops may require revisiting earlier phases
* Continuous integration and delivery enable frequent releases

In Product and Agile models, the lifecycle operates as an ongoing cycle rather than a one-time sequence.

---

### Integration with Change and Release Processes
The SDLC is tightly integrated with organisational Change and Release Management practices:

* All production deployments must follow approved change processes
* Release activities must align with environment and operational controls
* Traceability must exist between development activities and change records

This ensures that delivery speed does not compromise stability, security, or compliance.

---

### Embedded Governance and Controls
Governance is embedded throughout the lifecycle rather than applied only at specific checkpoints.

* Controls such as security, testing, and documentation are integrated into each phase
* Governance activities may appear as stage gates, reviews, or embedded practices depending on methodology
* Evidence of compliance must be maintained regardless of delivery approach

---

### Proportionality and Risk-Based Application
The level of formality and control applied across the lifecycle should be proportionate to:

* The risk and criticality of the system
* Regulatory and compliance requirements
* Complexity and scale of the solution
* Business impact of failure

Higher-risk initiatives require more rigorous controls, documentation, and oversight, while lower-risk efforts may adopt lighter-weight approaches—provided all mandatory outcomes are still achieved.

---

### Lifecycle Outputs and Traceability
Each phase of the SDLC produces key outputs that contribute to the overall integrity of the solution.

These may include:

* Requirements and backlog items
* Design artefacts and architectural decisions
* Source code and build artefacts
* Test cases and results
* Deployment records and release notes

All outputs must be:

* Appropriately documented
* Accessible to relevant stakeholders
* Traceable across the lifecycle

---

### Continuous Operation and Improvement
The lifecycle does not end at deployment. Ongoing activities include:

* Monitoring and incident management
* Performance optimisation
* Defect resolution
* Enhancements and new feature delivery

Insights gained during operation must feed back into discovery and planning to support continuous improvement.
